Over-reliance on AI in Consulting & Research

A practical perspective from research workflows

The Subtle Risk

AI tools are increasingly used in consulting and research workflows, particularly in secondary research where large volumes of information need to be synthesized quickly.

The primary risk is not incorrect answers alone, but the tendency to accept well-structured AI outputs without sufficient validation.

Where the Problem Shows Up

AI outputs are typically structured, coherent, and persuasive in tone. This can create a situation where outputs appear credible even when certain assumptions or data points are not fully validated.

Illustrative Examples

Market Trends

Statements such as “marine insurance premiums are rising globally” may be directionally plausible but can vary significantly by geography, route, or time period.

Industry Benchmarks

Quantified claims (e.g., efficiency improvements or cost reductions) may lack clear sourcing or context, despite appearing precise.

What Has Changed

Earlier challenge: lack of information

Current challenge: evaluating synthesized information that appears complete

Underlying Behaviour Shift

In practice, AI can lead to cognitive offloading — where outputs are treated as both a starting point and an endpoint, rather than an input to further analysis.

In my own workflow, I have observed this while synthesizing industry landscapes, where outputs appear comprehensive but still require validation at a granular level.

Practical Implication

AI is most effective as a synthesis and structuring tool, not a substitute for validation and judgment.

Key Takeaway

The risk is not that AI produces incorrect answers, but that well-presented outputs reduce the perceived need for verification.
