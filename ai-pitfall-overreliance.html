Over-reliance on AI in Consulting & Research

A practical perspective from research workflows

AI tools are increasingly used in consulting and research workflows — particularly in secondary research, where large volumes of information need to be synthesised quickly. The efficiency gain is real. But it comes with a risk that is easy to overlook.

The primary risk is not incorrect answers alone. It is the tendency to accept well-structured AI outputs without sufficient validation.

Where the problem shows up

AI outputs are typically structured, coherent, and persuasive in tone. This creates a situation where outputs appear credible — even when certain assumptions or data points have not been fully validated.

Illustrative Examples
Market Trends — Statements such as "marine insurance premiums are rising globally" may be directionally plausible but vary significantly by geography, route, or time period.
Industry Benchmarks — Quantified claims (e.g. efficiency improvements or cost reductions) may lack clear sourcing or context, despite appearing precise.

What has changed

Earlier Challenge
Lack of information — sourcing took time, and gaps were visible.
Current Challenge
Evaluating synthesised information that appears complete — gaps are no longer visible.

The underlying behaviour shift

In practice, AI can lead to cognitive offloading — where outputs are treated as both a starting point and an endpoint, rather than as an input to further analysis.

From my own workflow

While synthesising industry landscapes, I have observed this directly — outputs appear comprehensive but still require validation at a granular level. The structured format creates a false sense of completeness.

"The risk is not that AI produces incorrect answers — it is that well-presented outputs reduce the perceived need for verification."

Practical implication

AI is most effective as a synthesis and structuring tool — not as a substitute for validation and judgment. The more fluent the output, the more discipline the reader must apply.

Key Takeaway

Well-structured AI outputs make the absence of critical thinking less obvious — not less consequential.
