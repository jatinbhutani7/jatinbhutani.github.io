01Turning Points
1
Shami Dismantles SRH's Top Three — Overs 1–4
Shami, bowling against his former team, removed Abhishek with an offcutter in over 1 and Head with a slower ball in over 3. Prince Yadav then uprooted Kishan's off stump in over 4. SRH reached 11/3 after 4 overs — not a collapse of execution, but of top-order structure. Every specialist option at the top was gone before the powerplay ended. The innings had nowhere to go except a rescue mission.
2
Klaasen–Reddy Rebuild: 11/3 to 134/4 in 13 Overs
From 11/3 — a position where even 130 would have been a recovery — Klaasen and Reddy built a 94-run fifth-wicket stand, a new SRH record. In the final 30 balls of their partnership, they scored 79 runs with 7 fours and 5 sixes. The match was reversed mid-innings. At 134/4 after 17, SRH were looking at 175+. This partnership is what makes the subsequent collapse — and SRH's decision forks — analytically significant.
3
LSG's Death Execution: 5 Wickets for 22 in 4 Overs
From 134/4 after 17, SRH added only 22 runs and lost 5 wickets in the final 4 overs. Siddharth got Reddy caught at deep cover (over 18), Avesh then removed Klaasen with a reverse paddle miscued too fine, and had Dubey chopping on in the same over. Avesh gave away just 2 runs in over 19. The reverse swing was real — but LSG's bowling plan to attack the set batters at the death rather than simply contain was the decisive variable.
4
LSG 108/4 After 14 — Pant Alone With 49 to Get
Markram fell to a wrong'un at 10 overs. Badoni was stumped off Dubey. Pooran was run out in a miscommunication. Three very different dismissals in quick succession — but all while Pant was at the non-striker's end on 32 off 30. The wobble wasn't panic; it was partners taking risks that Pant couldn't manage. The question entering overs 15–20 was whether Pant could anchor the final phase. He had 16 overs of batting in his legs and knew exactly what the chase needed.
5
Pant's Last Over — 4, 4, 6 Off Unadkat
9 needed off the last over with Unadkat bowling. Full ball — Pant drilled straight for 4. Slow into the pitch — Pant swatted over mid-off for 4. Two dots. Then Pant launched the fifth ball over mid-off to win. What makes this decisive analytically: Pant had already hit Unadkat in overs 6 and 15. He had a read on the bowler. Unadkat's inability to change the plan — or SRH's unwillingness to change the bowler — was the final decision failure of the match.

02Phase Control
Powerplay
LSG
Dismantled
SRH's powerplay was structurally destroyed before it ended — 11/3, three specialist batters gone. Shami and Prince Yadav bowled to a clear plan: swing + slower balls + seam movement. SRH had no answer. The innings was a recovery mission from over 5 onwards.
LSG's bowling combination in the powerplay was exceptional. Shami's swing and pace variation (2/9 in 4 overs overall), Rathi's spin disruption, Prince Yadav's seam — three very different threats in 6 overs. LSG dominated both on wickets and on running rate restriction: SRH scored only 22.
Middle Overs
SRH
Rescued
Klaasen and Reddy turned a structural disaster into a competitive innings. 79 runs in the last 30 balls of their stand — a phase reclaimed by individual brilliance rather than team planning. In the chase, SRH bowling fought back well, taking 3 wickets in the middle (Markram, Badoni stumped, Pooran run out). The middle phase was genuinely contested across both innings.
LSG's middle-overs batting was fragile — Markram fell to a wrong'un, Badoni stumped, Pooran run out, all while Pant watched from the non-striker's end. SRH's bowling in the chase middle was sharp. The phase was contested — but Pant's anchor role meant LSG never lost complete control despite losing 3 wickets.
Death Overs
LSG
Decisive Loss
SRH's death overs were catastrophic — 5 wickets for 22 runs from 134/4. Then, in the chase death, Unadkat conceded 14 off the last over to a set Pant who had already read his plans. Both innings' death phases went decisively to LSG. SRH lost the match twice — once with the bat, once with the ball — in the same phase.
LSG's death-overs bowling in SRH's innings was the match's best phase of bowling — reverse swing, wicket-to-wicket line, attacking set batters rather than containing. In the chase, Pant's death-overs batting was equally decisive. LSG won both death phases. When a team wins the death twice, the match is rarely close.

04Match Decider
The moment that made the game
LSG's Death Bowling Converted SRH's Advantage Into a Manageable Target
SRH at 134/4 after 17 were on course for 175+. LSG's death overs — specifically Avesh with reverse swing, and Siddharth targeting Reddy at deep cover — reduced them to 156. That 19-run difference was the entire margin of the chase. Without it, Pant's heroics in the final over wouldn't have been enough. The match was won in overs 18 and 19 of SRH's innings, not in the last over of LSG's chase.
05Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
SRH lost this match in two death phases — once with the bat, once with the ball. A team that loses the same phase in both innings on the same day hasn't made two errors; it has revealed a structural vulnerability. Death-phase planning — who bowls, what length, which batter to attack, who anchors — was the defining gap between these two sides.
06Counter-Team Perspective

Where SRH had genuine decision forks — moments where an alternative was available, not just visible in hindsight — and what those alternatives would have required and cost.

Overs 17–19 · SRH Batting
Should one of Klaasen or Reddy have anchored rather than both pressing at the death?
+
At 134/4 after 17, SRH had three overs with two set batters who had scored 79 in the previous 30 balls. The natural instinct — press both — led to both falling in overs 18 and 19. Reddy caught at deep cover off Siddharth. Klaasen reverse paddling too fine off Avesh. A third batter also fell in the same over. SRH went from 134/4 to 146/7 in 2 overs.
What Happened
Both Reddy and Klaasen went for maximum in overs 18–19. Both fell to attacking shots that could have been avoided — Reddy to a conventional deep cover drive, Klaasen to an improvised reverse paddle. Five wickets fell in 4 overs. SRH finished on 156 instead of a likely 172–178.
The Available Alternative
One batter anchors (likely Klaasen, who was on 40 off 27 entering over 18) — rotating and taking singles — while Reddy goes for the boundary options. Losing one at the death is recoverable with the other set. The combined damage of losing both in sequence collapsed the innings' structural integrity and left the tail to face two overs.
What it would have cost: Marginally fewer sixes in overs 18–19. But if Klaasen stays to over 20 on 50–55, SRH likely add another 15–20 runs. At 172–178, Pant's last-over heroics don't win the match.
07Framework Insights
Losing the death phase twice in one match reveals structure, not bad luck
SRH lost the death with the bat (5 wickets for 22) and with the ball (14 off the last over). One death-phase failure is a match event. Two in the same game is a systemic pattern — death-over planning, batter protection, and bowler selection all need examination.
An exceptional partnership mid-innings can reverse a match — but it needs protection
The Klaasen-Reddy stand is one of the most valuable SRH partnerships this tournament. Its value was partially erased by the death collapse. A partnership rescue mission only changes the match if the batting side can protect one of the architects for the final phase.
Bowler familiarity is a real chase advantage — captains must account for it
Pant had faced Unadkat twice in this innings and read his variations. That read translated directly into the last over — 4, 4, 6. When a set batter has demonstrated they can score off a specific bowler, the bowling captain must either change the plan within that bowler or change the bowler. SRH did neither.
An anchor in a chase wobble is worth more than runs on the board
Pant scored 32 off 30 at the point of LSG's wobble — a strike rate that would look pedestrian in isolation. But his presence while three partners fell meant LSG never fully lost control. An anchor doesn't need to score fast when the team is wobbling — they need to still be there when the wobble ends.
