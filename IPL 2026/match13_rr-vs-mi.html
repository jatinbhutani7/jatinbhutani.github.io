01Turning Points
Format context: Match reduced to 11 overs a side, powerplay truncated to 3.2 overs. In this format, the powerplay is the match — 3.2 overs set the entire structural direction. Standard analytical weights shift: the powerplay carries disproportionate leverage, and a single over of 22 runs has the same relative impact as 4 overs in a full match.
1
Jaiswal–Sooryavanshi: 80 Runs in 4.6 Overs — Opening Partnership
Chahar's first over went for 22 (Jaiswal smashed). Bumrah's second over: Sooryavanshi hit two sixes but was run out. Boult's third over went for 22. In a truncated powerplay of just 3.2 overs, RR reached 59/0. The opening stand of 80 in 4.6 overs — Jaiswal 77* off 32, Sooryavanshi 39 off 14 (SR 278) — was the match. In an 11-over game, an 80-run first-wicket partnership covering nearly half the innings is the structural foundation no bowling attack can recover from.
2
Ghazanfar: 2/21 in 2 Overs — MI's Best Bowling Performance
After the powerplay carnage, Ghazanfar was MI's only bowler to consistently contain and take wickets. He dismissed Jurel (lbw, skiddy delivery) and Parag (caught long-on) to finish 2/21. His economy of 10.50 was the best among the wicket-takers. Without Ghazanfar, RR could plausibly have reached 165+. Even his 21 runs across 2 overs had a disciplined structure — the only MI bowler who didn't concede boundary-heavy overs in the RR innings.
3
MI's Powerplay Collapse: Three Wickets in 3.2 Overs — 30/3
Archer got Rickelton (over 1, top edge, hard length around off). Burger deceived SKY with a change of pace (over 2, caught at deep backward square leg). Sandeep bowled Rohit (over 3, yorker on middle after Rohit pre-committed to a fine paddle). Three completely different dismissals — no single pattern — meaning MI's top order had no shared structural problem, just individual execution failures. Chasing 151 in 11 overs at 13.7 per over with 30/3 after the powerplay is not a cricket problem. It is a mathematical impossibility without a single extraordinary innings.
4
Bishnoi Drops Rutherford — 25 off 8 at 312 SR Follows
Bishnoi should have had Rutherford caught at long-off first ball — a straightforward chance that Jaiswal shelled running in. Rutherford was reprieved on 0 and went on to make 25 off 8 at a strike rate of 312 (back-to-back fours off Bishnoi, then Deshpande for a four and two sixes). The drop didn't change the match result — MI were already 46/5 when Rutherford came in — but the difference between 93 and 123 for MI's total is largely attributable to that dropped chance. In a shortened format, every dropped catch has amplified consequences.
5
Sandeep Catches Rutherford Off Deshpande — MI's Last Hope Ends
MI's only realistic path to a competitive total required Rutherford to bat through to over 11. Deshpande bowled a slower hard-length ball outside off; Rutherford slashed and got a thick outside edge. Sandeep at short third dived forward and took a catch millimetres off the ground. A remarkable piece of fielding that ended Rutherford's innings at 25 off 8 and MI's last productive batting resource. From 93/6, MI finished 123/9 — the final 30 runs came from the tail in a lost cause.

04Match Decider
The moment that made the game
The Powerplay — Both Innings, Both Directions
RR won the powerplay batting (59/0 in 3.2 overs). RR won the powerplay bowling (MI 30/3 in 3.2 overs). In an 11-over match, the powerplay is not one phase among three — it is the match's structural foundation. Winning both powerplays produced an 89-run aggregate swing before either team had bowled or batted half their allotment. No middle-overs recovery or death-overs acceleration can overcome that deficit in the remaining 7.4 overs at 13+ per over.
05Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
In shortened formats, the powerplay is not a phase — it is the match compressed. The analytical framework of Powerplay / Middle / Death phases applies, but their relative weights shift fundamentally: 3.2 overs carry the leverage of 6 in a full game. Every team decision — toss, new-ball pairing, batting order, first-ball intent — must be evaluated against this compression.
06Counter-Team Perspective

Where MI had genuine decision forks — moments where an available alternative existed — and what those alternatives would have required and cost.

Over 1 · RR Batting
Should Bumrah have opened the attack instead of Chahar, given wet ball conditions neutralised Chahar's primary weapon — swing?
+
Chahar's primary asset is swing — an outswinger that troubles top-order batters in the early overs. In a match that had been delayed by 3+ hours of rain, with the ball wet from the start, the conditions that make Chahar dangerous were absent. Bumrah's primary assets — pace, hard-length accuracy, yorker control — are not condition-dependent in the same way. The commentary itself noted: "Did MI err by not giving Trent Boult the new ball? He is here bowling the third over with the ball already wet." The same question applies to Chahar in over 1.
What Happened
Chahar opened with a wet ball, conceded 22 in 1 over — Jaiswal smashed a half-tracker for 4, a six over midwicket, a cut over point, a four over mid-off, and a cover drive. 22/0 after 1 over. Bumrah bowled 3 overs in total for 0/32 (economy 10.67) — his best economy was in the conditions that followed the powerplay, not the powerplay itself.
The Available Alternative
Bumrah opening means Jaiswal faces him cold, in over 1, before settling. In these specific conditions — wet ball, night game, no swing — Bumrah's hard-length and pace is the most dangerous new-ball option MI had. Even if Bumrah concedes 12–14 in over 1, that is 8–10 fewer runs than Chahar conceded. In an 11-over match, 8 runs across one over changes the chase calculation materially.
What it would have cost: Bumrah opened instead of Chahar means Chahar comes in later — against a more settled Jaiswal. Chahar's economy was 22 per over anyway; bowling him later does not obviously cost more. The trade is clear: MI's best bowler in the conditions MI were playing in should have had the new ball.
Over 3 · MI Batting
Rohit pre-committed to a fine paddle against Sandeep — the bowler who had dismissed him five times before — and was bowled by a yorker on middle. Should he have trusted his natural game?
+
Rohit entered over 3 with MI already 22/2, chasing 151 in 11 overs at 13.7 per over. He played two dot balls — 83 SR from an opener who needed to be at 150+ from ball one — then attempted a fine paddle against Sandeep Sharma, who had dismissed him five times in 12 previous IPL innings. The paddle pre-commitment meant Rohit moved across his stumps early. Sandeep read it completely and nailed a yorker on middle. Rohit reviewed. Ball tracker: middle of middle.
What Happened
Rohit played 2 dot balls at 83 SR, then tried a fine paddle — an improvised shot against a bowler who has repeatedly dismissed him. He pre-committed and moved across his stumps. Sandeep recognised it immediately and bowled a yorker on middle. Plumb lbw. Rohit out for 5 off 6, MI 22/3. The chase was statistically over before Hardik faced his first ball.
The Available Alternative
Against a bowler with a known history of dismissing you, the last thing to do is pre-commit to an unconventional shot. Rohit's natural game against pace — driving straight, pulling the short ball — is better suited to Sandeep's lengths than a paddle-sweep. Attacking intent from ball one through his natural arc would have been both more productive and less likely to be dismissed in this specific way. Sandeep cannot bowl a yorker every delivery.
What this reveals: Rohit's dismissal wasn't unlucky — it was a predictable outcome of pre-committing against a bowler who knows exactly how to dismiss him. The fine paddle against a seamer bowling yorkers, with a wicketkeeper up to the stumps, requires near-perfect timing. Against Sandeep specifically, with 5 previous dismissals in the record, attempting an improvised shot is giving the bowler what he wants — the batter pre-moving before the ball is bowled.
07Framework Insights
In shortened formats, the powerplay is the match — the other phases are consequence
59/0 batting and 30/3 bowling in 3.2-over powerplays produced a combined 89-run swing before over 4 of either innings. No team recovers from losing both powerplays in 11 overs. Standard phase analysis must recalibrate: powerplay weight in an 11-over game approximates 55% of total match leverage.
Bowler selection must account for conditions, not just capability
Chahar is a capable bowler. But in wet-ball conditions where swing is neutralised, his primary threat is removed. The selection decision — new ball pairing — must be conditional on the actual match environment, not the bowler's general profile. Bumrah's hard-length accuracy works in all conditions.
Prior dismissal patterns create exploitable pre-commitments
Sandeep had dismissed Rohit 5 times in 12 innings. Rohit pre-committed to a paddle — exactly the shot a bowler with that record would plan for. When a bowler has repeated success against a batter, the batter's adjustment must come before the shot, not after. Rohit's paddle attempt gave Sandeep the early information he needed.
Dropped catches in short formats have amplified financial cost
Jaiswal dropped Rutherford on 0; Rutherford made 25 off 8. In a full game, one dropped catch rarely defines the result. In 11 overs, a single batter scoring 25 off 8 represents a significant portion of the total innings — 20% of MI's total came from the drop. Short-format catching standards must be treated as execution-critical, not routine.
