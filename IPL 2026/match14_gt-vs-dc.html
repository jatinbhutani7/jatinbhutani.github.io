01Turning Points
1
Buttler's Powerplay Blitz — 47+ in 15 Balls, GT 68/1 After 6
For the first time in three IPL 2026 innings, Buttler's full range appeared. Two sixes off Axar, three off Mukesh in over 3 alone — a scoop, a straight hit, and a lofted cover drive. In five overs, Buttler contributed 47+ of GT's 68 powerplay runs. Sudharsan was the only casualty. By the end of the powerplay, GT's total trajectory had been set firmly above 200. Buttler was eventually out for 52 off 27 when a Kuldeep long hop kept low — the wrong shot to a poor ball, but the damage was done.
2
Ngidi's Masterclass: 4-0-24-1, Economy 6.00
In a match where almost every bowler conceded at 10+ per over, Ngidi finished with 4-0-24-1 — economy of 6.00. His pace-off strategy was specific and consistent: 17 balls at reduced pace, 16 runs, 1 wicket (Gill). 9 balls at full pace, 8 runs. The change of pace forced mistimings and restricted the boundaries that other DC bowlers conceded freely. Without Ngidi's 4 overs, DC's total concession would have been 20+ higher — erasing any plausibility of a close finish entirely.
3
Phillips Drops KL Rahul at 21 — Rahul Finishes 92 off 52
Glenn Phillips ran 20-25 metres backwards and had the ball in his hands before it popped out. Rahul had toe-ended a half-slap to covers — the kind of catch that must be caught. The drop cost DC a minimum of 71 runs (Rahul's eventual total minus his score at the time). Rahul's 92 off 52 was the foundation of DC's entire chase — without it, the match isn't close. This is an execution failure, not a decision fork, but its consequence was directly structural to the result.
4
Rashid Khan: Rana and Rizvi in Consecutive Balls — DC 101/3 After 10
DC were 101/2 and progressing. Rana holed out to long-off (101/2). Next ball: Rizvi bowled by a wrong'un he didn't pick (101/3). Two wickets on consecutive deliveries off Rashid ended any middle-overs momentum DC had built. Axar then came in with DC needing 81 off 42, but Miller had retired hurt — DC were suddenly thin on batting resources at the point where the chase needed its key phase to begin. Rashid's final figures: 4-0-17-3, economy 4.25. In a match where GT's bowlers averaged 13+ per over, this was match-altering.
5
Rahul Run Out Stubbs, Then Gets Out Next Ball — 166/6 After 17
Rahul called Stubbs for a run then refused it. Sai Sudharsan fired a direct hit. Stubbs run out for 7. Then on the very next delivery, Rahul nicked a wide Siraj yorker behind square — out for 92 off 52. Two wickets in two balls off his own batting. DC went from 160/5 to 166/6 in 6 deliveries. The run-out was Rahul's call error; the dismissal was an attacking shot on a wide ball. Together they swung the match firmly to GT — DC now needed 45 off 18 with Miller (injured), Vipraj Nigam, Kuldeep, and the tail.
6
Miller Returns, Takes Siraj for 6-4-6 — DC Need 13 Off Last Over
Miller had retired hurt (bruised thumb, 12* off some balls at 130/3) and returned when Stubbs was run out at 160/5. With DC needing 36 off 12, he deposited Siraj for 6, 4, 6 in an over that went for 19 — delivering a performance that would have been extraordinary for a fully fit batter, let alone one batting with a bruised thumb. He brought DC to 13 needed off the final over. Then, with 2 needed off 2, he refused a single — setting up the last ball drama that GT survived.

DC couldn't prevent the powerplay from setting GT's trajectory above 200. In the chase, DC were excellent: 63/0 from Nissanka and Rahul, platform intact.

Rashid alone won this phase for GT.

04Match Decider
The moment that made the game
Rashid Khan's Over 10 — Two in Two, Match Turned
DC were 101/2 and on track. Rashid removed Rana (101/2) and Rizvi (101/3) on consecutive deliveries — a wrong'un both couldn't pick. Miller had already retired hurt. DC went from a chase requiring 9.6 per over with two set batters, to the same target with the middle order scrambled and their best middle-order batter off the field. Rashid's 4-0-17-3 (economy 4.25) in a match where most bowlers conceded 13+ is the stat that best explains a 1-run result. Without those 3 wickets for 17, DC win comfortably.
05Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
A 1-run match is decided by the accumulation of small decisions — a single over from the wrong bowler, a single refused at the wrong moment. DC lost by 1 run. Axar's unused over cost 12. Miller's refused single cost a tie at minimum. Neither decision looked obviously wrong at the time. That is what makes 1-run defeats analytically important: the margin reveals the decisions that were invisible when they were made.
06Counter-Team Perspective

DC lost by 1 run. Both forks below are directly quantifiable against that margin.

DC Bowling · GT Innings
Axar Patel bowled only 3 of his 4 allocated overs. His unused over was replaced by Vipraj Nigam's only over — which cost 23 runs. DC lost by 1.
+
DC's bowling allocation: Mukesh Kumar 4, Ngidi 4, Axar 3, Kuldeep 4, Natarajan 4, Vipraj Nigam 1 — totalling 20 overs. Axar had one over remaining in his quota. Vipraj Nigam's single over cost 23 runs. Axar's 3 overs cost 32 (economy 10.67 — approximately 10-11 runs per over). The arithmetic is direct: replacing Nigam's over with Axar's 4th over saves approximately 12 runs. DC lost by 1.
What Happened
Axar bowled 3 overs — his last over was not used. Vipraj Nigam was given the remaining over and conceded 23 (economy 23.00). Nigam is primarily a batting option; his bowling is emergency cover. In a match where DC needed every run they could save, giving a non-specialist one full over was a tactical cost that proved decisive.
The Available Alternative
Axar's 4th over at his match economy of 10.67 would have cost approximately 10-11 runs. Against Nigam's 23, the saving is 12 runs. DC lost by 1. This is not hindsight — it was available information during the match. Axar was not injured or restricted from bowling his full allocation. The decision to withhold his 4th over is unexplained by the match narrative.
Quantified cost: Approximately 12 runs — directly greater than the margin of defeat (1 run). This is the rarest kind of analytical finding in a close match: a decision with a quantifiable cost that exceeds the loss margin, verifiable from the scorecard alone.
Last Over Ball 5 · DC Batting
With 2 needed off 2 balls, Miller refused a single that would have left DC needing 1 off 1 with Kuldeep on strike. He then missed ball 5; Kuldeep was run out off the last.
+
DC needed 13 off the last over. After 4 balls, they had scored 11 — requiring 2 off 2. Miller was on strike. A single was available off ball 5. Taking it would have left Kuldeep needing 1 off 1 to win — or any wide, no-ball, or bye to tie and force a super over. Miller refused, presumably intending to win it himself. Prasidh then bowled a slower bumper, Miller swung and missed. Ball 6: Kuldeep, backing up, was run out by Buttler's underarm flick.
What Happened
Miller refused the single off ball 5. He needed 2 off 1 ball to win — a boundary. Prasidh denied him with a slower bumper. Miller missed. Off the last ball, Kuldeep was backing up and Buttler completed a direct hit underarm to run him out. DC finished on 209. GT won by 1 run.
The Percentage Decision
Taking the single: 1 needed off 1 for Kuldeep. Any error by Prasidh — a wide, a no-ball, a full toss — and DC tie or win. Kuldeep only needs to get bat on ball for 1 run. Even a leg-bye or a run-out attempt gone wrong ties the match. The number of ways DC win or tie off 1 ball for Kuldeep is higher than the number of ways Miller hits a boundary off a Prasidh slower ball.
The decision logic: Miller at 200+ SR was confident he could hit the boundary. That confidence is understandable — he had just taken Siraj for 6-4-6. But Prasidh had one ball and could bowl anything. The percentage calculation — multiple paths to a tie via the single vs one path to a win via Miller's boundary — clearly favoured taking the run. Miller's decision to back himself was human; the probability was against him.
07Framework Insights
1-run matches are decided by the decisions that looked harmless at the time
Axar's unused over and Miller's refused single were not obvious errors in the moment. They were small decisions that, in isolation, appeared inconsequential. In a 1-run match, the accumulation of small decisions is the match. The system's framework must account for these moments — they are the real "turning points" of close games.
Bowling quota management is a quantifiable decision variable
Axar's unused over is the clearest example this tournament of a quota decision with a directly measurable cost. When a bowler has remaining overs and their economy is lower than the replacement, using their full quota is the percentage play. Exceptions exist (saving a batter for a chase pressure situation) — but Axar batted for 3 balls and scored 2 runs. The exception did not apply here.
The percentage of a last-ball decision depends on what options remain
Miller's decision to refuse the single was not wrong in isolation — he was a batter in form. It was wrong relative to the alternatives: taking the single opened multiple paths to a tie or win. The correct framework for last-ball decisions is not "can I hit this" but "what is the probability distribution of all available options."
Rashid Khan is the most valuable phase-control bowler in this dataset
4-0-17-3 in a 210-run match, taking wickets that dismantled the chase at its pivot point. Rashid's economy and wicket-taking in Match 14 is the standout individual bowling performance in 14 matches. His wickets weren't lucky — each was a premeditated wrong'un or variation. That combination of wickets + control is what the system defines as phase dominance.
