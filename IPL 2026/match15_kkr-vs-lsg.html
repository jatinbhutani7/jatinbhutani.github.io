01Turning Points
1
Rahane–Raghuvanshi: 84-Run Stand, Then Both Fall in 12 Balls
KKR's second-wicket partnership of 84 runs in 52 balls (Rahane 41 off 24, Raghuvanshi 45 off 33) was the platform their innings needed. But the manner of their dismissals in quick succession defined the match's middle phase. Rahane dragged a Rathi delivery to midwicket — playing against the turn against a legspinner — and was caught by Shami for 41. Then Raghuvanshi advanced against Siddharth and was caught at long-on by Markram. KKR went from 99/1 to 105/3 in two overs. At 99/1 after 10, a total of 200+ was possible. The structural collapse that followed — Rinku adding only 4 off several balls before being bowled — left KKR's middle-overs run rate stalled at the exact moment the innings needed to press — Green struggling against spin and Rinku unable to get going.
2
Arora's Double Strike — Markram and Marsh in the Same Over
KKR's Vaibhav Arora (Impact Player) removed both LSG openers in over 4 (4.1 and 4.3). Markram miscued a drive to Powell at 41/1; Marsh got the faintest of gloves down leg — so faint the keeper had to come around — and was caught at 42/2. Two very different dismissals, two balls apart. LSG had been on 31/0 after 3 overs and building with intent. The double-wicket over immediately swung momentum to KKR and put Pant under pressure entering an innings that required him to rebuild rather than accelerate.
3
KKR's Spin Choke: LSG 42/2 to 128/7 in 70 Balls
The match's defining phase. After KKR's Arora removed both openers, KKR's spinners took complete control. Green dismissed Pant (73/3, top-edged hook), Tyagi got Pooran (95/4), Roy bowled Samad (104/5) and caught Badoni via Rinku at long-off (125/6), Narine had Shami sweeping to the deep (128/7). Six wickets in 70 balls — the most sustained middle-overs bowling phase in this 15-match dataset. At 128/7 needing 54 from 4 overs, no team recovers from this position. Except Choudhary hadn't batted yet.
4
Choudhary: 54* off 27, 7 Sixes — From 104/5 to Win
Mukul Choudhary entered at 104/5 with LSG needing 78 off 8 overs. What followed is the most statistically extraordinary lower-order innings in this dataset — 54 off 27 with 7 sixes, including 53 runs in the last 5 overs alone (only Pollard in 2013 has scored more at the death in a successful IPL chase). His helicopter six over long-on off Tyagi, described as reminiscent of Dhoni by du Plessis on commentary. A 6,4,6 sequence off Green in over 19 turned a needing-30-off-2 situation into needing-14-off-1. First-ever 50+ at this position in a successful run chase by an LSG batter.
5
Green Bowls Over 19 — Hit for 6,4,6 — Match Flipped
Cameron Green had not bowled a single ball in IPL 2026 before this match. KKR gave him the 19th over of a tense chase, with Choudhary in full flow needing 32 off 12. Choudhary hit him for 6 (helicopter over long-on), 4, then 6 — 18 runs off the over. LSG now needed 14 off the last instead of a near-impossible 30+. Green's bowling had been anticipated throughout the tournament but its first use came at the highest-pressure moment. Dale Steyn and Aaron Finch publicly criticised the decision post-match.
6
Last Ball: Choudhary Misses, Scampers Leg-Bye — Raghuvanshi's Throw Misses
14 needed off Arora's final over. Choudhary got to 7 off 4 balls (one six, one wide yorker scythed over point). With 1 needed off 1 ball: Arora bowled a slower bouncer, Choudhary swung and missed, both batters sprinted. Avesh scrambled through for a leg-bye. Raghuvanshi's throw to the striker's end missed. LSG won by 3 wickets. The last-ball miss is an execution failure — Raghuvanshi took a split second extra to line up his throw. The structural position (1 needed off 1 for LSG with 3 wickets down including Green having bowled an 18-run over) was entirely of KKR's making.

04Match Decider
The moment that made the game
Green Bowling Over 19 — A First-Timer in the Highest-Pressure Moment
KKR needed to defend 32 off 12 balls with Choudhary in full flow. They gave the 19th over to Cameron Green — making his first IPL bowling appearance of the season, having not bowled a single delivery across three previous matches. Choudhary hit him for 6, 4, 6. Eighteen runs off one over swung the match from near-impossible to genuinely alive. Without it, LSG needed 30 off 6 balls — statistically, the match was over. With it, they needed 14 off 6, with Choudhary on strike. The decision to bowl Green there is the clearest bowling selection failure in this 15-match dataset.
05Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
KKR dominated every phase of this match — powerplay batting, middle-overs bowling, innings total — and still lost. The reason is not close: a single bowling decision in over 19 erased the platform built across 18 overs. This is the system's clearest evidence yet that death-phase execution can negate all prior phase control. KKR's 0 wins in 4 games cannot be attributed to poor planning; it is being attributed to execution failures at the phases that decide close games.
06Counter-Team Perspective

Where KKR had genuine decision forks — and what the alternatives would have required.

Over 19 · LSG Chase
KKR gave Green the 19th over — his first IPL bowling spell of the season — with Choudhary in full flow needing 32 off 12. He conceded 18. The decision was criticised publicly by Dale Steyn and Aaron Finch.
+
Green had not bowled a single delivery across KKR's three previous IPL 2026 matches. His bowling fitness had been a talking point all tournament. Giving him the 19th over of a tense chase — not a low-pressure test in the middle, but the most consequential over with a set, in-form finisher on strike — is the highest-risk context for a first bowling spell. The risk wasn't Green's ability; it was the timing. A batter who has hit three recent sixes against a bowler they're facing for the first time has every advantage.
What Happened
Green bowled over 19, conceding 6, 4, 6 off three deliveries to Choudhary (18 runs total). LSG's required total fell from 32 off 12 to 14 off 6. The 18-run over converted an unlikely chase into a competitive finish. Steyn and Finch both stated post-match it was the wrong decision — Green's first IPL bowling appearance should not have been the 19th over of a close chase.
The Principle
A bowler's first spell in a high-pressure situation should never come in the match's decisive over. KKR's other bowling options — Saini (experienced), Tyagi — had already bowled. Narine is the question: if he had overs remaining, he should have bowled over 19. Regardless of who the specific alternative was, the principle is clear: introducing an untested bowler into the highest-leverage moment is not a calculated risk — it is an avoidable one.
What this reveals: The decision suggests KKR may have planned to use Green in lower-pressure overs and then ran out of options at the death. That planning failure — not accounting for Green's over-19 availability — is the root cause. Whether it was a contingency plan gone wrong or a deliberate choice, the consequence was 18 runs in a match KKR should have won.
Overs 11–14 · KKR Batting
Green scored 6 off 11 balls against spin in the middle overs. Once his struggle was visible, did KKR make any tactical adjustment to protect him until pace returned?
+
Green entered at 99/2 after Rahane's dismissal. Against Rathi and Siddharth in the middle overs, he managed 6 off 11 balls — visibly uncomfortable against spin. This is not surprising: Green is a pace-hitter whose power comes from hard-length balls he can drive or pull. Spin in the 10–14 over range, with fielders set for the aerial shot, is his weakest zone. Yet KKR made no observable adjustment — no attempt to shield him by farming the strike differently, no change in the batting instruction from the dugout. He eventually scored freely once pace returned in the death overs (finishing 32 off 24), which confirms the issue was phase-specific, not a general form problem.
What Happened
Green scratched around for 6 off 11 against Rathi and Siddharth with no tactical adjustment from KKR. Raghuvanshi, playing his natural game, advanced and was caught at long-on. KKR slid to 105/3, then 111/4 with Rinku managing only 4 off several balls. KKR's middle-overs run rate stalled precisely when their batting structure was best placed — two batters in, Powell saved — to push.
The Tactical Adjustment
Consciously farming the strike — keeping Green away from Rathi and Siddharth by taking singles off the last ball of each over, or targeting the non-spinning options more aggressively. This is not a batting-order change; it is an in-match adjustment that experienced batting partnerships make when one batter is clearly in a difficult matchup. Raghuvanshi, comfortable against spin, takes the spinner. Green targets pace when it comes. The same two batters, different strike management.
What this reveals: KKR's batting structure in the middle overs was passive — they played the ball on its merits rather than actively managing an obvious mismatch. Green scoring 32 off 24 at the death proves he was not in poor form; he was in the wrong phase against the wrong bowler type. Protecting him for 3–4 overs until pace returned was available and wasn't taken. The additional runs from a better-timed Green could have put KKR at 195+ — a total that changes the calculus of Choudhary's final over entirely.
07Framework Insights
The first clear counterexample: a team can recover from losing the middle overs
The system's thesis — losing the middle overs means losing the match — is challenged directly by Choudhary's innings. LSG lost 6 wickets in 70 middle-overs balls, were 128/7, and still won. The counterexample's caveat: they needed a historically rare lower-order innings (only two precedents in IPL history for that death-overs scoring rate). The thesis holds as a probability statement, not an absolute rule.
A bowler's first spell should never come at the match's highest-pressure moment
Green's over-19 appearance was his first IPL bowling in 2026. Regardless of his ability, introducing an untested bowler in the 19th over of a close chase is an avoidable risk. The timing of a bowler's introduction must account for match context — not just their skill level. KKR's planning failure was in not having a clear death-bowling hierarchy that didn't require Green at this moment.
KKR's 0-from-4 record reveals a pattern, not a run of bad luck
KKR have lost four matches. In three of them (this dataset), they lost in the death phase despite controlling earlier phases. A team that consistently wins the platform battle and loses the execution battle has a structural problem — not a tactical one. Death-overs planning: bowler allocation, batter protection, over assignment — is KKR's defining gap in IPL 2026.
A set batter's shot selection at the middle-to-death transition is a specific risk category
Raghuvanshi's dismissal (advancing against a spinner with a long-on in place) and Rahane's (dragging against the turn to midwicket) both occurred in the same 2-over window. Both were attempts to accelerate against spin — both chose the wrong shot type for the specific field and bowling angle. Acceleration intent is correct; the method of acceleration must be matched to the specific delivery and field.
