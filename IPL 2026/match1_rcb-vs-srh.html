01 Turning Points
1
Duffy's First Spell
Early damage, not a collapse. SRH lost three attacking options in the first five overs, shifting the innings from multiple threats to dependence on one player. The wickets created a perception of control that SRH couldn't convert into actual phase dominance.
2
Kishan Out at 80 — 16th Over
The defining moment. He was set to control the death overs. Once he fell, SRH had hitters but no one controlling the innings. A batter's removal at the death is not about runs lost — it is about tempo lost. SRH had no one to set the rhythm for the final phase.
3
Kohli Dropped at 28
The only real opening in the chase. Once missed, the game became straightforward. In a chase built on controlled accumulation, removing the set batter resets the tempo — RCB never had to find that reset.

04 Match Decider
The moment that made the game
RCB's Powerplay with the Bat
That 70+ start removed scoreboard pressure and forced SRH out of attacking options early. From there, the chase followed a set path — no risk required, no pressure phase reached. A powerplay in a chase doesn't just build runs; it eliminates entire categories of risk.
05 Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
SRH didn't react quickly enough in the powerplay. It wasn't just personnel, but predictability. Same lengths, limited field adjustments, and no attempt to break Padikkal's rhythm allowed the phase to drift — and drift, left uncorrected, becomes the match.
06 Framework Insights
Early wickets need follow-through
Taking wickets is step one; controlling the next phase is what matters. SRH took wickets in both innings — and converted neither into a control phase.
Powerplay in a chase defines the game
Getting ahead early removes the need for risk later. RCB's 70+ start made every subsequent over a formality — no pressure phase was ever reachable.
Set batter at the death is the biggest asset
Kishan's wicket mattered because of timing, not just value. A settled batter at the death doesn't just add runs — they prevent opposition from recalibrating the game.
Pressure must be actively created
If the game drifts, you need to force change — field, pace, or angle. SRH didn't. Passive field settings and predictable plans allowed drift to become defeat.
