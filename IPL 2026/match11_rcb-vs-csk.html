01Turning Points
1
Dube Drops Kohli at 7 — 21 Runs Cost, Tone Set
Khaleel drew a miscued shot from Kohli on a back-of-length delivery. Dube at long-on got into an awkward side-on position and shelled it. Kohli went on to make 28 off 18. The drop didn't just cost 21 runs — it cost CSK the momentum of dismissing RCB's anchor before the innings had rhythm. Kohli's subsequent six and four changed the crowd and the match's tempo. The drop is an execution failure, but the context matters: CSK had no other early wicket to show for a disciplined powerplay start.
2
RCB 91/1 After 10, Then 159 Runs in Last 10 Overs
The match's defining structural fact. RCB at the halfway point looked like a 185–190 total — competitive but chaseable. What happened in overs 11–20 was categorical: Padikkal accelerated to 50 off 29, then Patidar-David dismantled the attack at strike rates of 252 and 280 respectively. Tim David faced zero balls in the first 14 overs and scored 70* off 25. The back-loading of RCB's innings was extreme by any standard — the highest-ever IPL total against CSK in the league.
3
Duffy Removes Both CSK Openers in First 3 Overs
Gaikwad edged to slip in over 1. Samson edged to slip in over 3. Both caught by Padikkal. CSK were 10/2 after 8 balls chasing 251. The chase needed a platform immediately — instead, the two most experienced top-order batters were gone before the powerplay had found its shape. Duffy's length — hard into the pitch, moving away late — was identical for both dismissals. CSK showed no adjustment between the first and third overs.
4
Sarfaraz 50 off 24 — Then Stumped First Ball After Powerplay
Sarfaraz's powerplay innings was the only moment CSK looked like they had a plan. 50 off 24 with 8 fours and 2 sixes — he read the conditions better than anyone else in the CSK lineup. Then, on the first ball of over 7 with the powerplay fielding restrictions lifted, he charged at Krunal Pandya and was stumped by a mile. CSK went from 77/3 — still theoretically in the chase — to 77/4 immediately. The set batter, the one who had played the conditions well, was gone through self-inflicted aggression at the worst possible moment.
5
Veer–Overton Partnership: 57 Runs in 26 Balls
From 108/6 after 9.5 overs, Veer (43 off 29) and Overton (37 off 16) built a 7th-wicket stand of 57 that gave the score cosmetic respectability. Both batters played with freedom — nothing to lose, strike rates above 145. This is a recurring CSK pattern in 2026: the tail and lower middle order batting better than the top order because the game situation removes the pressure of the chase. Overton's 37 off 16 in particular showed what an unfettered lower-order batter can do against tired bowling.

Slow Surface
RCB reached 51/1 off the powerplay — decent but not dominant. The tacky surface and slow outfield constrained both sides. Kohli's drop at 7 was costly. CSK's bowling found swing and threatened edges. RCB were building without controlling — the phase belonged to neither team decisively.
CSK's powerplay bowling was their best phase. Khaleel found swing, Henry was disciplined, and they nearly had Kohli twice. But the drop changed everything — 51/1 instead of a potential 30/2 is a very different foundation. In the chase, CSK's powerplay was catastrophic: 30/3 in 3 overs, Sarfaraz the only batter to play the conditions.

RCB
Building Phase

RCB controlled the phase without dominating it — they were building towards the death, not trying to win it in the middle.
After Sarfaraz's stumping at 77/4, CSK lost Kartik at 84/5 and Dube at 108/6. The middle overs dismantled what little the powerplay had preserved. Three wickets between overs 7 and 10 — no phase, no anchor, no plan. CSK ceded the middle entirely after their best batter was needlessly dismissed.

CSK's death bowling had no answer for Patidar-David. Noor conceded 49 (0 wickets) across his 4 overs, taking three consecutive sixes from David. No field setting was relevant when David was clearing the rope from any length. In the chase death, CSK were all out for 207 — the Veer-Overton stand was the only resistance.

04Match Decider
The moment that made the game
Patidar–David: 99 Runs in 6 Overs From a Position of 151/2
RCB at 151/2 after 14 overs were heading for 185–190. The Patidar-David partnership added 99 runs in 44 balls — Patidar at SR 252, David at SR 280. David faced zero deliveries in the first 14 overs and still top-scored. No CSK bowling combination had an answer: Noor took three consecutive sixes from David. The partnership didn't just score runs — it removed the chase from feasibility entirely, turning a competitive game into an impossibility before the first ball of the second innings.
05Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
CSK lost this match in two separate moments of self-destruction: Sarfaraz stumped first ball after a match-saving 50, and both openers caught in the same slips cordon off identical Duffy deliveries. RCB didn't manufacture these dismissals — CSK gifted them. The technical gap between these teams was real, but the margin of defeat was amplified by decisions within CSK's control.
06Counter-Team Perspective

Where CSK had genuine decision forks — moments where an alternative was available and actionable — and what those alternatives would have required and cost.

Over 18 · RCB Batting
Kamboj had the right ball to David — a yorker that beat him completely — then overstepped. A wicket became a free-hit six.
+
Every CSK bowler got hit by David — Noor (three sixes), Overton (30 in one over), Khaleel, Henry. This is not a bowler selection problem. When a batter clears the rope regardless of who bowls, the question shifts: what length consistently troubles this batter? David generates power through arm speed and thrives on full deliveries. Back-of-length, hard into the pitch, is harder for him to get under. Kamboj found exactly that ball — a yorker from around the stumps that had David beaten — and then overstepped.
What Happened
Kamboj executed the right delivery — a yorker that beat David completely. He celebrated. No-ball called. Free hit. David clubbed it for six over long-on. A wicket became a six — a minimum 12-run swing in the context of the death overs. The plan was correct. The ball was correct. The run-up was not.
What Controlled Execution Looks Like
The same delivery, landed legally. David is out at the crease, RCB lose a set batter in the 18th over with Patidar still there. The partnership breaks, the momentum shifts, the final total is likely 15–20 runs lower. The solution to David existed — Kamboj demonstrated it. The only variable was a foot placement under pressure.
What this reveals: CSK had no systematic answer to David, but they had one moment where the correct answer materialised — and execution discipline eliminated it. At the highest level, the difference between a plan working and failing is sometimes a single foot placement. That is not a consolation; it is the standard T20 death bowling requires.
Over 7 · CSK Batting
Should Sarfaraz have anchored through at least one more over after reaching 50, given CSK were already 77/3 chasing 251?
+
Sarfaraz reached 50 off 24 with the powerplay ending. The required rate was around 21 per over — practically impossible. But the only path to any kind of competitive position was a set batter continuing to score at his powerplay rate through the middle overs too. Instead, he charged Krunal on the first ball after the powerplay break and was stumped by a significant margin.
What Happened
Sarfaraz charged first ball of over 7, missed the drive against Krunal's turning delivery, stumped by Jitesh Sharma. 77/4 immediately. The only set batter who had read the conditions was gone through an aggressive shot that had no situational justification — CSK needed him to bat longer, not faster.
The Available Alternative
Sarfaraz had already demonstrated he could score at 200+ strike rate in the powerplay. Staying in for overs 7–12 while rotating and hitting the bad ball — rather than charging a spinner on ball one — keeps the asking rate notionally alive and forces RCB to bowl their good balls. The chase was unlikely regardless. But 77/3 at over 7 with a set batter is a fundamentally different match than 84/5.
What it would have cost: Accepting a slightly lower strike rate for 2–3 overs — perhaps 150 instead of 200+ — while preserving wickets. The powerplay had already been played at maximum aggression; the middle overs required a different tempo. Sarfaraz's exit collapsed not just his own innings but the entire structural logic of the chase.
07Framework Insights
A back-loaded batting lineup changes what the middle overs mean
RCB at 91/1 after 10 looked like a 185–190 team. Tim David hadn't batted. The presence of a genuinely destructive finisher means middle-overs run rates are not the story — they are merely the setup. Bowling captains must plan for who is coming, not who is batting.
The set batter's dismissal tempo matters more than their total
Sarfaraz's 50 off 24 was exceptional. His stumping on ball one of over 7 negated it almost entirely. The value of a set batter in a steep chase isn't their score — it's their continued presence. Sarfaraz scoring 70 off 40 in a losing cause is worth more strategically than 50 off 24 then out.
Identical dismissals in the same over signal a bowling plan, not luck
Gaikwad and Samson both edged Duffy to the same slip fielder off similar lengths in overs 1 and 3. Duffy had a clear plan — back of a length, angled away — and CSK batters showed no adjustment. When a bowler takes two slips catches in three overs, the batting team must change something. CSK didn't.
Lower-order batting fluency in a lost cause reveals top-order anxiety
Veer hit 43 off 29 and Overton 37 off 16 with freedom — because the game was gone and pressure had lifted. This is now the third CSK match where the lower order outperforms the top. The gap isn't talent; it's the pressure distribution that the chase structure creates on batters who should be anchoring.
