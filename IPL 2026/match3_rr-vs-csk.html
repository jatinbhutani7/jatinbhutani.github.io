04Match Decider
The moment that made the game
RR's Powerplay Bowling Plan
The match was decided by preparation, not reaction. RR's bowlers arrived with a conditions-specific plan — specific lengths, field settings calibrated to the pitch — and executed it before CSK could set their own agenda. In T20, the team that controls the powerplay's terms usually controls the match.
05Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
Games are sometimes decided by one team arriving prepared and the other not. CSK's powerplay wasn't a collapse — it was a drift. Drift without correction becomes the match. The difference between preparation and adaptation is the difference between controlling a phase and reacting to losing one.
06Framework Insights
Conditions reading at over 1 is a decisive skill
Teams that adjust to pitch, dew, and carry from the first ball remove the opposition's ability to set tempo. RR didn't react to conditions — they anticipated them.
Passive drift compounds faster than aggressive failure
CSK's powerplay didn't feature collapses — it featured dots. Passive dot balls in the powerplay compound exponentially as the required rate rises each over.
Total must be structurally threatening, not just large
RR's total was effective because it demanded CSK play above their powerplay failure. The number mattered less than what it required of the chase.
Defending teams control games before the chase starts
RR's advantage was established in their own innings. The chase was a formality — confirmation of an advantage built phases earlier.
