The moment that made the game
MI's Death Overs Collapse
MI controlled the middle overs but couldn't convert. Their death overs execution collapsed — no set batter, predictable bowling, no phase discipline in the final stretch. DC's middle-overs execution had already set the chase terms; MI's death made it easier.
05Decision Insight
Core analytical finding
Control without conversion is the most frustrating form of defeat. MI structured the game well through the middle and then let it unravel at the death. DC's execution in the middle overs set the chase terms — and MI's death-overs failure made those terms achievable.
06Framework Insights
Middle-overs control without death execution is incomplete
MI owned the middle but not the death. Phase control is cumulative — losing the final phase negates prior work more than any other phase combination.
Set batter at the death is non-negotiable
MI's death collapse was partly structural — no batter positioned to anchor the final phase. Death-overs planning starts in the middle, not at over 16.
DC's middle precision set the chase terms
DC didn't just survive the middle overs — they dominated them. Their batting and bowling execution in the pivot phase determined what the chase would require.
Converting control is a distinct skill from acquiring it
MI demonstrated they can build control. The gap was in converting it — death-overs execution, bowling discipline, and phase continuity. Acquisition without conversion is insufficient.
