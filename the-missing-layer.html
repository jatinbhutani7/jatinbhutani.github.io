Insurance AI · India
April 2026

The missing
layer

India's insurance AI gap is not what most people think it is.

India has world-class AI talent. It has a fast-growing insured population and a regulatory regime increasingly open to innovation. Yet AI-powered underwriting remains almost entirely confined to motor and simple retail lending products. The assumption in most discussions is that this is a technology or talent problem. It is neither.

The bottleneck is structural. The data exchange layer — the infrastructure that would allow AI models to be trained on real, representative, population-scale data — does not yet exist at the depth required. India has built identity infrastructure (Aadhaar) and payments infrastructure (UPI) to world-beating scale. The data layer is the third pillar, and it remains underdeveloped.

This piece maps 10 insurance AI use cases against India's current data exchange readiness — identifying what is commercially viable today, what is close, and what remains structurally blocked. The purpose is not to catalogue problems but to locate exactly where the opportunity sits when the infrastructure catches up.

"The algorithms are ready. The models exist. What is missing is the data infrastructure to feed them."

Implications
01

The Account Aggregator proves the model works

In financial services — where AA infrastructure is live — AI-powered lending, credit scoring, and wealth management have advanced rapidly. Flow-based lending to SMEs is already emerging. The same logic, applied to health and agriculture, would unlock markets of comparable scale. The infrastructure, not the algorithm, was the constraint.

02

Health is the highest-value unsolved problem

Preventive health insurance and dynamic health underwriting represent the largest single opportunity — and the deepest structural gap. ABDM enrollment is growing but thin. Private hospital data remains fragmented. Until a functional health data exchange exists, India's health insurers will continue pricing on proxies rather than evidence, underwriting bluntly in a market that rewards precision.

03

Agriculture is a policy failure, not a technology failure

The data required to build accurate crop parametric models exists — satellite imagery, mandi transaction records, IMD weather data, soil profiles. It sits in government systems that are not commercially accessible. NDGF, if operationalised, would change this overnight. The 300 million farmers exposed to weather risk are waiting not for better AI, but for a government decision to open the data.

04

The opportunity is largest for challengers, not incumbents

Established insurers have legacy pricing models calibrated to existing data. When data exchange infrastructure matures, the companies positioned to gain most are those building underwriting models now — before the data arrives — so they can train rapidly when it does. First-mover advantage in insurance AI is about model architecture readiness, not just data access.
