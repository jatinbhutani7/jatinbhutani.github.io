Insurance AI · India · April 2026

The missing layer

India's insurance AI gap is not what most people think it is. The bottleneck is not talent, technology, or regulatory appetite. It is data infrastructure — the exchange layer that would allow AI models to be trained on real, representative, population-scale data.

India has world-class AI talent. It has a fast-growing insured population and a regulatory regime increasingly open to innovation. Yet AI-powered underwriting remains almost entirely confined to motor and simple retail lending products. The assumption in most discussions is that this is a technology or talent problem. It is neither.

The bottleneck is structural. India has built identity infrastructure (Aadhaar) and payments infrastructure (UPI) to world-beating scale. The data exchange layer — the third pillar — remains underdeveloped. Without it, AI models cannot be trained on real, representative, population-scale data across health, agriculture, or complex commercial lines.

"The algorithms are ready. The models exist. What is missing is the data infrastructure to feed them."

Key Insights
01
The Account Aggregator proves the model works

In financial services — where AA infrastructure is live — AI-powered lending, credit scoring, and wealth management have advanced rapidly. The same logic, applied to health and agriculture, would unlock markets of comparable scale. The infrastructure, not the algorithm, was the constraint.

02
Health is the highest-value unsolved problem

Preventive health insurance and dynamic health underwriting represent the largest single opportunity — and the deepest structural gap. ABDM enrollment is growing but thin. Private hospital data remains fragmented. Until a functional health data exchange exists, India's health insurers will continue pricing on proxies rather than evidence.

03
Agriculture is a policy failure, not a technology failure

The data required to build accurate crop parametric models exists — satellite imagery, mandi transaction records, IMD weather data, soil profiles. It sits in government systems that are not commercially accessible. NDGF, if operationalised, would change this overnight. 300 million exposed farmers are waiting not for better AI, but for a government decision to open the data.

04
The opportunity is largest for challengers, not incumbents

Established insurers have legacy pricing models calibrated to existing data. When data exchange infrastructure matures, the companies best positioned are those building underwriting models now — before the data arrives — so they can train rapidly when it does. First-mover advantage is about model architecture readiness, not just data access.
